Over the past five decades, women have steadily surpassed men across the U.S. educational system. Today, women earn 58% of all bachelor's degrees. Among recent high school completers, 65.3% of women enroll in college, compared to 57.6% of men. At institutions such as MIT and Caltech, women are now admitted at nearly twice the rate of men.

Source: U.S. Department of Education (AEI / Carpe Diem)

This educational shift is now reaching the labor market. Of the 369,000 jobs created in the past year, 348,000 — nearly 95% — went to women. Women now hold more than half of all U.S. jobs, and nearly 80% of positions in the fastest-growing sector of the economy: health care.

Sources: NCES Digest of Education Statistics (2023); NPR / U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (2026)

Over the past five decades, the U.S. energy sector has undergone significant structural changes. Coal, which once accounted for more than half of all electricity generation, has declined steadily as a share of the national energy mix. In 2022, coal represented 20% of U.S. electricity generation, down from a peak of over 52% in the early 1980s.

Share of U.S. electricity generation by source, 1970–2026
(coal vs. natural gas)
Natural gas Coal Projected
Source: U.S. Energy Information Administration

This shift has been driven primarily by the falling cost of natural gas extraction following the expansion of hydraulic fracturing technology in the 2000s. In 2016, natural gas surpassed coal for the first time as the leading source of U.S. electricity. By 2022, natural gas accounted for 38% of total generation. The EIA projects this trend to continue through the end of the decade.

Source: U.S. Energy Information Administration, Electric Power Annual (2023)
